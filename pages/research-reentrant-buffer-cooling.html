Scheduling of Single-Arm Multicluster Tools for Multi-Type Wafers with Parallel Processing, Residency Time Constraints, and Reentrant Buffer Cooling Operations

This project, which has been running from November 2025 to the present, develops an online scheduling policy for single-arm multicluster tools processing multiple wafer types under parallel processing, residency-time constraints, and reentrant buffer cooling operations. I serve as the overall project lead, and the work is organized as a full decision pipeline from formal problem definition to latency-aware evaluation.

The pipeline starts with a precise problem definition and an event-driven simulator. The simulator reproduces robot transfers, process-module operations, buffer-module waiting, cooling behavior, and time-window constraints, so each candidate decision can be tested against the physical evolution of the manufacturing system.

On top of the simulator, the project builds a legal action generator and constraint checker. This component enumerates only feasible scheduling actions and verifies resource exclusivity, route precedence, residency-time limits, reentrant buffer cooling requirements, and deadlock-sensitive interactions before an action is passed to the optimization or learning layer.

The optimization layer combines CP-SAT, large neighborhood search, heuristic methods, and rule-constrained reinforcement learning. These methods are used as complementary solvers and policy sources: CP-SAT supports exact feasibility reasoning, large neighborhood search repairs critical regions, heuristics provide fast rule-based decisions, and rule-constrained reinforcement learning explores higher-quality choices without violating hard constraints.

To support learning and analysis, the system maintains a hierarchical evidence bank. This evidence bank records simulator states, feasible actions, constraint-checking outcomes, solver traces, repair decisions, and evaluation feedback, giving the later representation and policy modules structured evidence rather than isolated raw events.

The state representation is built with a heterogeneous graph state encoder, followed by Mamba or Transformer process memory. The graph encoder represents robots, process modules, buffer modules, wafer lots, legal actions, and constraint relations as typed nodes and edges, while the process memory captures long-range temporal dependencies in the evolving schedule.

The decision module uses multi-head value and risk estimation. The heads estimate V(s) for remaining makespan and state quality, Q(s,a) for legal action value, Risk(s,a) for residency, cooling, and deadlock risk, DeltaC(s,a) for predicted makespan change, and Macro-Q(s,o) for macro-option value. These estimates allow the policy to balance short-term feasibility, long-term schedule quality, and operational risk.

The policy layer uses masked reinforcement learning and hierarchical reinforcement learning. Action masks prevent illegal decisions from being selected, while hierarchical decisions allow the policy to choose both fine-grained actions and higher-level macro options for larger scheduling moves.

Before execution, each selected decision passes through runtime validation and local repair. The final output is an online scheduling policy that can be evaluated through main results, ablation studies, generalization experiments, and latency tests, ensuring that the method is not only feasible and high-quality but also practical for real-time or near-real-time scheduling scenarios.
